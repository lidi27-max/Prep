Strategy refresher

Before the quiz — review these patterns

These are the four traps that trip up even prepared listeners. Read each one, then see real examples from a previous talk.

The 4 key strategies

Context ≠ main topic

If the professor uses one concept to introduce another, the topic is the second one. Ask: what does the professor keep returning to throughout the whole talk?

If it wasn't said, it's not the answer

For detail questions, eliminate anything logical but not stated. TOEFL penalizes reasonable inferences the audio doesn't directly support.

Analogies illustrate a concept

When the professor uses an everyday example, ask: what academic concept is this being used to explain? The example itself is never the point.

Examples show general properties

If something specific is mentioned (a plant, a place), the conclusion is the general property that example demonstrates — not the specific thing itself.

Real examples — chemistry talk (catalysts)
Q5Main idea
What is the main topic of the talk?
BThe role of catalysts in chemical reactions ✓ correct
AHow a chemical reaction progresses from start to finish common trap
CMaterials used in the production of nanozymes
DThe importance of enzymes in digestion
The professor mentions how reactions work as a starting point — but the whole talk circles back to catalysts. The main topic is always what the professor keeps elaborating on, not what they use to open.
Why A is tempting: reactions are mentioned from the very beginning, so they feel central. The trap is confusing the setup with the main topic.
Q6Detail
What point does the professor make about the unchanging nature of catalysts?
CIt allows them to be used repeatedly. ✓ correct
AIt makes them easier to produce in laboratories. common trap
BIt causes them to lack flexibility.
DIt makes them inefficient for some complex chemical processes.
The logic the professor states: catalysts aren't consumed → therefore they can be reused. That's the direct connection in the audio.
Why A is tempting: "if it doesn't change, it must be easier to make" feels logical — but the professor never says it. If it's not in the audio, it's not the answer.
Q7Rhetorical purpose
Why does the professor mention a cake?
ATo show how catalysts make reactions occur with less energy ✓ correct
DTo explain how chemical reactions can influence cake flavor common trap
BTo point out a common problem with digestion
CTo illustrate how certain enzymes produce high temperatures
The cake is an analogy: baking powder helps it rise without extreme heat → just like a catalyst lowers activation energy. Focus on the concept the example illustrates, not the example itself.
Why D is tempting: cake + chemistry sounds like flavor. But everyday examples in academic talks always illustrate the concept being taught — they are never the main point.
Q8Inference from example
What does the example of environmental cleanup reveal about nanozymes?
CThey perform reliably in extreme settings. ✓ correct
DThey are most effective at breaking down plastics. common trap
AThey are expensive to produce.
BThey can be found abundantly in nature.
The cleanup example demonstrates a general property — nanozymes work in harsh, extreme conditions. The conclusion is always the property revealed, not the specific substance or setting mentioned.
Why D is tempting: plastics were probably mentioned in the example, making them feel like the key takeaway. But the example shows durability in extreme conditions — that's the general property.
Ask yourself before every answer
Did the professor say this directly, or am I inferring it?
If there's an example, what concept was being explained right before it?
For main idea: which option covers the whole talk, not just one part?
Am I picking this because it sounds right, or because the professor explicitly connected it?
